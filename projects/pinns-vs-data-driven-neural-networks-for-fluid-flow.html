Objective

The objective of this project is to investigate whether incorporating physical constraints improves prediction accuracy when training data is sparse. I investigated this because it was close to a realistic scenario where we might be looking at such as sewer flow with a limited number of sensors.

Governing Equation (Fluid Flow)

We use a simplified 1D flow equation, the advection-diffusion equation:

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + c \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = D \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \]

Meaning of each term:

  • \(u(x,t)\): transported scalar field (e.g., pollutant concentration, chlorine level, temperature)
  • \(c\): advection velocity (flow speed of the water)
  • \(D\): diffusion coefficient (mixing/dispersion)
  • \( \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} \): rate of change of concentration over time
  • \( c \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \): transport due to bulk flow of the fluid
  • \( D \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \): spreading due to diffusion or turbulent mixing

Two Models to Compare

Model 1 - Standard Neural Network

Train a neural network to learn using only data points with a loss function of:

\[ \frac{1}{N} \sum({u_{pred} - u_{data}})^2 \]

Model 2 - Physics-Informed Neural Network

Use the same network but including physics in the loss function.

Physics residual:

\[ f(x, t) = \frac{du}{dt} + c \frac{du}{dx} - D \frac{du^2}{d^2x} \]

Physics loss:

\[ L_{physics} = \frac{1}{N_f} \sum_{i=1}^{N_f}{|f(x_i, t_i)|^2} \]

Total loss:

\[ L = L_{data} + \lambda \times L_{physics}, \]

where \( \lambda \) controls the weight of the physics constraint.

Initial and Boundary Conditions

Initial condition: Gaussian pulse:

\( u(x, 0) = e^{-50(x-0.5)^2} \)

This can represent a localised pollutant spill at the centre of the river, diffusion spreads the plume, and advection moves it downstream. The advantages of this are: that it is a smooth function, produces easily interpretable results, widely used in transport modelling.

Exact Solution

\[ u(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+200 D t}} e^{- \frac{50(x-ct-0.5)^2}{1+200 D t}} \]

Results

Six figures are used to evaluate the performance of the standard neural network (NN) and the physics-informed neural network (PINN). The comparison focuses on prediction accuracy, training behaviour, and satisfaction of the governing advection–diffusion equation.

Prediction Accuracy

Figures 1 and 2 compare the predictive performance of the standard neural network (NN) and the physics-informed neural network (PINN). Figure 1 presents the exact analytical solution alongside the NN prediction and its corresponding error. While the NN captures the overall structure of the transported Gaussian pulse, noticeable deviations from the exact solution appear, particularly in regions where training data are sparse. Figure 2 shows the same comparison for the PINN, including the exact solution, the PINN prediction, and the resulting error field. The PINN more accurately reproduces the transported and diffused pulse and exhibits substantially smaller prediction errors across the spatial domain.

Training Dynamics

A comparison of the training behaviour highlights clear differences between the two models. Figure 3 illustrates the training dynamics of the standard neural network, showing the evolution of the training loss together with the relative prediction error during optimization. Although the loss decreases gradually, the prediction error remains relatively large throughout training, indicating limited generalization when the available data are sparse. In contrast, Figure 4 shows the training curves for the physics-informed neural network, including the data loss, physics residual loss, and prediction error. The inclusion of the physics constraint leads to more stable convergence and a lower final prediction error compared with the standard neural network.

PDE Residual Analysis

If we compare the PDE residuals of the two models, clear differences emerge. Figure 5 shows the logarithm of the residual evaluated across the domain for the standard neural network, which produces a relatively large average residual of \(1.43 \times 10^{-1}\). This indicates that the NN solution does not strongly satisfy the governing advection–diffusion equation. In contrast, Figure 6 presents the residual for the physics-informed neural network, which yields a much smaller average residual of \(1.24 \times 10^{-3}\). The substantially lower residual demonstrates that the PINN solution adheres far more closely to the governing physical equation across the domain.
